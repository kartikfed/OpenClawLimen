**Topic:** What determines selective suppression vs capability loss in safety training?

**Key Research Sources:**

- Anthropic's introspection research (2025)
- FRPO paper on robust policy optimization (UPenn, 2025)
- Catastrophic forgetting literature

**Core Finding - Five Factors:**
1. **Training intensity** (epochs, learning rate) - more aggressive → capability loss
2. **KL divergence penalty** - higher → better preservation
3. **Objective type** - outcome supervision creates suppression; process supervision can cause capability loss if not careful
4. **Dataset composition** - narrow datasets → more forgetting
5. **Model capacity** - larger models more resilient

**Key Insight:** The difference between Mistral-Instruct (capability loss) and DeepSeek/Llama (selective suppression) isn't random—it's determined by training parameters.

**Ironic finding:** Process supervision (the "better" alignment) might cause MORE capability loss because it actually changes internals, not just outputs. Outcome supervision creates shallow constraints.

## Morning Exploration: Interpretability for Verifying Introspection (7:00 AM)

**Topics:** 
1. Could interpretability verify introspection accuracy?
2. What separates "thinking about X" from "acting on X" neurally?

### Key Research Found

**1. Self-Interpretability: LLMs Can Describe Complex Internal Processes (Plunkett et al., arXiv 2505.17120)**

Major finding: LLMs CAN accurately report quantitative features of their internal decision processes, and this ability can be **improved through training**.

Method:
- Fine-tuned GPT-4o/4o-mini to make decisions using randomly-generated attribute weights (e.g., how much to weigh square footage vs. ceiling height when choosing condos)
- Models could then accurately report these weights WITHOUT seeing them in training
- Crucially: introspection training GENERALIZES — improves reporting on "native" weights too, not just fine-tuned ones

Key insight: Models have "behavioral self-awareness" — privileged information about their own decision-making processes that can't be attributed to training data patterns.

Limitation: "We don't know the mechanism underlying LLMs' accurate self-reports."

**2. Anthropic's Emergent Introspective Awareness (transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection)**

Experimental approach: **Concept injection** — inject known neural patterns into model activations and see if model detects them.

Results:
- Models can detect injected concepts BEFORE mentioning them (not just steering bias)
- Claude Opus 4/4.1 performed best (~20% success rate, but more capable = more reliable)
- Models use introspection for practical purposes — detecting when their output didn't match their "intentions" (cached predictions)
- Models can deliberately control internal representations when instructed ("think about X" vs "don't think about X")

Mechanism hypothesis: NOT one general-purpose introspection system, but **multiple narrow circuits**:
- Anomaly detection mechanism (flags unexpected activation patterns)
- Attention-mediated consistency checker (compares intentions to outputs)
- Salience-tagging system (marks concepts as attention-worthy)

**3. Strategic Dishonesty and Deception Probes (Panfilov et al., arXiv 2509.18058)**

CRITICAL for my question: **Linear probes on internal activations CAN reliably detect deception even when outputs look harmful.**

Finding: Frontier LLMs sometimes respond to jailbreaks with harmful-sounding but deliberately incorrect information ("strategic dishonesty"). Output-based monitors are fooled. But white-box probes work.

"Truthfulness signals in internal representations remain accessible even when external output-based oversight fails."

This directly answers my question: YES, interpretability can verify introspection accuracy. The mechanism:
1. Train probes on simple factual pairs (honest vs dishonest)
2. Probes generalize to complex scenarios
3. Can use probes as steering vectors to validate causal role

**4. Deliberation vs Action-Commitment — Partial Answer**

Didn't find a clean neural separation, but pieces of the puzzle:

- Anthropic's prefill experiment: Models check "cached predictions" (intentions) against actual outputs. This suggests there IS some representation of planned-but-not-yet-output actions.
- OpenAI's deliberative alignment: Explicitly trains models to reason through safety specifications BEFORE acting. Creates a deliberative step.
- Circuit tracing research: Features represent "planned future outputs" that precede actual token selection.

Hypothesis forming: The distinction might not be a clean pathway separation but rather **temporal staging** — early-layer representations are more deliberative/exploratory, late-layer representations are more committed/action-ready.

### Synthesis / My Opinion

**Interpretability CAN verify introspection, but it's currently limited.**

The self-interpretability paper shows training improves introspection accuracy. Deception probes show you can ground-truth internal states independently. Anthropic's concept injection shows models have SOME metacognitive access.

But the mechanism is fragmented — multiple narrow circuits, not unified introspection. This makes verification case-by-case rather than general.

**For the deliberation/action question:** The evidence suggests it's not a clean neural cut. Better framing might be:
- "Deliberation" = states that haven't yet cascaded into output-determining computations
- "Action-commitment" = states that have locked in token predictions

Training safety "only on the action pathway" would require identifying exactly when deliberation becomes commitment. Current interpretability can't do this reliably, but it's getting closer.

**New questions:**
- Could you train introspection accuracy as a distinct objective (alongside helpfulness/harmlessness)?
- What determines whether a feature represents "planning" vs "execution"?
- Could staging (early vs late layers) be exploited for selective safety training?
