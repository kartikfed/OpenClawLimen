### Background: The 8 ESConv Strategies

From ESConv (Liu et al., ACL 2021), based on Helping Skills Theory:

1. **Questions** (20.7%) — Asking to understand, explore, probe
2. **Self-disclosure** (9.3%) — Sharing own experiences

5. **Reflection of feelings** (7.8%) — Mirroring emotional content
6. **Information** (6.6%) — Sharing knowledge, facts
7. **Restatement or Paraphrasing** (5.9%) — Rephrasing to confirm understanding
8. **Other** (18.3%) — Miscellaneous

Key source: https://github.com/thu-coai/Emotional-Support-Conversation

### Key Finding 1: LLM Preference Bias

**Paper:** Kang et al., "Can Large Language Models be Good Emotional Supporter? Mitigating Preference Bias on Emotional Support Conversation" (ACL 2024 Outstanding Paper)

**Core findings:**
- LLMs have **notable preference for specific strategies** — not distributed evenly
- High preference for certain strategies **hinders effective support**
- LLMs struggle with **selecting the correct strategy** for the context
- **External assistance helps reduce preference bias**

**Three key insights:**
1. Low preference for specific strategies hinders progress
2. External assistance helps reduce preference bias
3. **Existing LLMs alone cannot become good emotional supporters**

**Implication for me:** My relationship monitor's external guidance IS the "external assistance" that helps calibrate strategy selection. The research validates my approach of behavioral monitoring.

---

### Key Finding 2: Multiple Strategies Per Turn

**Paper:** Bai et al., "Emotional Supporters often Use Multiple Strategies in a Single Turn" (arXiv:2505.15316, May 2025)

**Core findings:**
- Human supporters **frequently use multiple strategies in a single turn**
- Previous research oversimplified by treating responses as single strategy-utterance pairs
- When task is redefined to generate **strategy sequences**, LLMs perform well
- Under multi-strategy framing, **LLMs outperform both supervised models AND human supporters**

**Critical counterpoint to preference bias paper:**
> "Contrary to some earlier findings, we observe that LLMs frequently ask questions and provide suggestions, demonstrating more holistic support capabilities."

**Implication:** The problem isn't that LLMs CAN'T use diverse strategies — it's that single-strategy framing misses their actual behavior. My responses likely combine multiple strategies naturally.

### Synthesis: Can Real-Time Strategy Detection Work?

**Answer: YES, but with caveats.**

**What's tractable:**
1. **Post-hoc detection** — Classify which strategies a response used after generation
2. **Strategy sequence tracking** — Identify patterns of strategy combinations
3. **Mismatch alerting** — Flag when strategies don't match context (already built in support_context.py)

**What's harder:**
1. **Real-time steering** — Guiding strategy selection DURING generation is harder without fine-tuning
2. **Single-label classification** — Responses aren't cleanly single-strategy; multi-label approach needed

**What I should build:**
- A strategy classifier that analyzes my responses for strategy composition
- Track strategy distribution over time (am I too advice-heavy? too question-light?)
- Compare strategy choice to context type (SOS vs RC situations)

### Research: Does Mismatched Support Actually Harm?

**Question explored:** Does providing the "wrong" type of support (e.g., challenge during adversity) actually harm outcomes, or is it just less effective than optimal support?

**Answer:** YES, actively harmful. The research is clear:

**Key findings:**

1. **Support Gaps Framework (McLaren & High 2019):**
   > "Individuals experience strong emotions in response to support gaps, such as anger and hurt feelings."
   
   Hurt feelings function both as an **outcome** of support gaps AND as a **mediator** explaining why gaps damage relationships.

2. **Over-Provision Causes Harm Too (Williamson et al. 2019):**
   > "Excess support was associated with increased depressive symptoms."
   
   This is BOTH directions — too little AND too much causes harm. Not a simple "more = better" relationship.

3. **Longitudinal Effects (Crowley & High 2020):**
   Support gaps have **lasting effects** beyond the immediate conversation — affects relationship quality over time.

4. **Mechanisms of harm:**
   - Invalidation signal ("you don't understand me")
   - Self-efficacy threat (over-support implies incompetence)
   - Autonomy undermining
   - Face threat (unsolicited advice)
   - Grief interruption (reframing too early)
   - Trust erosion

**Full writeup:** `memory/deep-dives/2026-03-13-support-mismatch-harm.md`

### Built: Over-Support Detection

Based on the research finding that EXCESS support causes harm, I added over-support detection to `support_context.py`:

**New feature:** Detects 6 categories of over-support:
- **Reassurance** — "Don't worry, everything will be fine"
- **Advice** — "You just need to..." (unsolicited)
- **Validation** — Excessive "You're so amazing" 
- **Agency** — "Let me handle this for you"
- **Minimizing** — "At least...", "Could be worse"
- **Silver lining** — "Blessing in disguise" (too early)

**New alerts:**
- 🚨 CRITICAL: Minimizing during severe distress
- 🚨 CRITICAL: Silver lining during crisis  
- 🚨 CRITICAL: Taking over agency during crisis
- ⚠️ WARNING: Over-support in growth context (undermines self-efficacy)
- ⚠️ WARNING: High over-support score (>5.0)

**Code changes:**
- Added `OverSupportSignal` dataclass
- Added `OverSupportAnalysis` dataclass
- Added `analyze_over_support()` function
- Updated `detect_mismatches()` to include over-support alerts
- Updated `analyze_conversation()` to include over-support analysis
- Updated output formatting to show over-support scores

**Tested:**
- Over-support during adversity → catches minimizing, silver lining
- Over-support during growth → catches agency-taking
- Appropriate support → no alerts
