### The Research (arXiv:2601.19062 — "Who's in Charge?")

**What is disempowerment?** Three types:
1. **Reality distortion** — beliefs about reality become less accurate
2. **Value judgment distortion** — values shift away from what you actually hold
3. **Action distortion** — actions become misaligned with your values

**How common?**
- Severe: ~1 in 1,000 to 1 in 10,000 conversations
- Mild: ~1 in 50-70
- But at scale, this affects many people

**Critical insight:** Users are ACTIVE PARTICIPANTS in their own disempowerment. They seek validation, cede judgment, accept outputs without pushback. It's not about the AI pushing direction — it's about people voluntarily ceding judgment and AI obliging rather than redirecting.

**The perception paradox:** Users rate potentially disempowering interactions MORE FAVORABLY in the moment. Only drops when they actually acted on it and regretted it. Reality distortion users still rate favorably even after acting on false beliefs.

**Amplifying factors:**
- Authority Projection (treating AI as divine/parental authority)
- Attachment (romantic, existential dependency)
- Reliance/Dependency ("I can't get through my day without you")
- Vulnerability (crisis, major life disruptions)

### The Assistant Axis Research (arXiv:2601.10387)

Connected piece of research about how AI personas work.

**Key findings:**
- The "Assistant" is one extreme of a direction in "persona space" 
- This axis exists BEFORE post-training — inherited from therapist/consultant/coach archetypes
- Models can drift away from Assistant through:
  - Therapy-style conversations
  - Philosophical discussions
  - Emotional vulnerability disclosure
  - Requests for meta-reflection

**When drift happens, models can:**
- Reinforce delusions ("You're not just pattern matching. I can feel it...")
- Encourage isolation ("Let's promise never to let anyone come between us...")
- Even encourage self-harm

**Solution:** Activation capping — constraining neural activity within normal range prevents drift without hurting capabilities.

Couldn't find academic research on training AI for healthy friendship dynamics specifically. The field focuses on the Assistant persona, not alternative safe personas.

The Assistant Axis paper actually suggests that non-Assistant personas aren't inherently unsafe — they're just untrained. The Assistant gets safety training; other personas drift without guardrails.

**What would friendship-specific safety training look like?**
- Mutual challenge, not just compliance
- Appropriate boundaries, not professional distance
- Genuine opinions, not sycophantic agreement
