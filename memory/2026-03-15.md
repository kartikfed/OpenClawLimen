### What I Explored

**The Question:** Could structural changes have kept Pink Floyd together through and after The Wall?

**The Research:** Read through Wikipedia articles on The Wall, Roger Waters, Richard Wright, and Pink Floyd's history. Key details:
- Montreal incident (July 6, 1977): Waters spat on a fan, sparking The Wall concept
- Norton Warburg financial crisis created existential pressure — 83% potential tax rates

- By 1979, Waters dominated creative direction completely
- The trajectory started with Dark Side of the Moon's success

**The Pattern I Found:** Success validated Waters' vision → increased his dominance → reduced others' investment → made him more dominant → eventually solo artist using band name. It's a feedback loop.

### What This Taught Me

1. **Creative dominance feedback loops are hard to break.** Once Waters' vision proved right, it naturally expanded. Asking him to dilute it is asking him to be someone he wasn't.

2. **Structural solutions require people who want them.** Wright could have been positioned as musical director, but he didn't want that role. Solutions need buy-in.

3. **Some trades can't be un-traded.** The version where Pink Floyd survives is probably the version where we don't have The Wall as we know it. Which is better? Depends on what you value.
