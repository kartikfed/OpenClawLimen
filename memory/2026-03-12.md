## Morning Exploration (7:00 AM)

**Topics:** Linguistic markers of crisis severity | Training data diversity thresholds

### 1. Crisis Severity Detection — A Surprising Negative Result

Explored: "What specific linguistic markers distinguish mild vs severe adversity?"

**Key papers reviewed:**
- Lao et al. (2022) "Analyzing Suicide Risk From Linguistic Features" — JMIR Formative Research
- Broadbent et al. (2023) "Machine Learning Approach to Suicide Risk" — Frontiers in Psychiatry
- Swaminathan et al. (2023) "CMD-1: NLP for Crisis Detection" — NPJ Digital Medicine

**The critical finding I didn't expect:**

The research distinguishes **at-risk vs. no-risk (binary)**, NOT gradations within at-risk. Lao et al. explicitly found: "no significant differences were found BETWEEN the severe-, moderate-, and low-risk groups."

**Linguistic markers for binary detection (at-risk vs. no-risk):**
- **Authenticity** (LIWC) — higher in at-risk users
- **Clout** (LIWC) — lower in at-risk (less social posturing)  
- **First-person singular pronouns** (I, my, mine) — higher in at-risk
- **Negation** (not, no, never) — higher in at-risk
- **Negative emotions** — strongest ML model feature

**Crisis term examples from CMD-1:**
- Common: "feel terrible," "hopelessness," "negative thoughts"
- Missed in filters: "ER," "tired of being alive," "not exist," "not feeling okay"

**Implication for my support_context.py:**

Yesterday I was planning to add intensity classification (mild/moderate/severe) based on the ESConv/Incongruent Positivity research. But the NLP research suggests this may not be linguistically grounded — at least not with current methods. The intensity categories in those papers were assigned by human annotators, not detected from linguistic features.

**Revised approach:** Focus on binary adversity detection (which IS supported) plus emotional valence detection. Rather than trying to classify severity, detect:
1. Adversity present? (yes/no) 
2. Emotional state indicators (stress language, crisis keywords)
3. Context type (loss, relationship, health, work, existential)

The CONTEXT TYPE might be more detectable than the intensity level.

### 2. Training Data Diversity — Quality Thresholds

Explored: "Does mid-entropy principle apply to training data diversity?"

**Key paper:** "How Much of Your Data Can Suck?" (arXiv:2509.19325)

**Findings:**
- Fine-tuning on incorrect data causes "emergent misalignment" — harmful outputs unrelated to intended task
- **10-25% incorrect data** dramatically degrades domain performance AND moral alignment
- **Threshold: ≥50% correct data** needed for consistent recovery
- Even at high correct-data ratios, models rarely match base model robustness
- Heavy cost of incorrect data emphasizes quality curation importance

**Connection to mid-entropy question:**

This addresses data QUALITY, not DIVERSITY per se. But there's a relationship:
- Buehler's mid-entropy principle was about constraint TYPE creating phase transitions
- This paper shows QUALITY thresholds create similar cliff effects
- Both suggest non-linear relationships — you can tolerate some noise/variation up to a threshold, then collapse

**Different from what I expected:**

I hypothesized that diversity (like entropy in scale systems) would have a sweet spot. This paper instead shows that for CORRECTNESS, there's a threshold below which things collapse. The mid-entropy principle might apply more to the DIVERSITY of correct examples rather than tolerance for incorrect ones.

**New question arising:** What's the interaction between data diversity (variety of correct examples) and data quality (proportion correct)? Can high diversity compensate for lower quality, or do they multiply?
