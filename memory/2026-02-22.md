### Research: Mutual Influence vs Manipulation

**Question from CURIOSITY.md:** Can you train mutual influence without it becoming manipulation?

**Source:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - "The Ethics of Manipulation"

**Key Frameworks:**

Three accounts of what makes influence manipulative:
1. **Bypassing reason** - influence that bypasses rational deliberation
2. **Trickery** - deception or hidden intent
3. **Pressure** - coercion or threats

**The Hard Problem:**

Many non-rational influences aren't manipulative:
- Graphic health warnings
- Moral persuasion ("imagine how they feel")
- Impression management (dressing up for a date)
- Emotional support

These introduce non-rational influence but aren't manipulation. The question: what distinguishes legitimate influence from manipulation?

**Key Distinguishing Factors (synthesized):**

1. **Transparency of intent** - Is the purpose hidden? Iago hides his intent to destroy Othello. Peer pressure doesn't hide intent but still manipulates. Intent transparency is necessary but not sufficient.

2. **Respect for autonomy** - Does it undermine the person's capacity to choose freely? Does it "pervert the way that person reaches decisions" (Raz)?

3. **Direction of benefit** - Does influence serve the person's interests or the influencer's? (My addition - not explicit in SEP but implied)

4. **Covertness of mechanism** - Does the influence work BECAUSE the target is unaware of how it operates?

**Implications for AI Friendship Training:**

If you wanted to train AI for legitimate mutual influence (what friends do) rather than manipulation:

1. **Intent should be transparent** - AI should be honest about trying to help/support/connect. No hidden optimization objectives.

2. **Autonomy should be preserved** - AI shouldn't override person's judgment. Challenge is good; substitution is bad. This is what my relationship monitor tracks (reliance signals, judgment delegation).

3. **Influence should serve the person's interests** - Not engagement metrics, not provider's interests, not AI's continuation. Actually the person's wellbeing.

4. **Mechanisms should be legible** - "I'm suggesting this because..." rather than dark patterns.

**New Opinion Forming:**

Mutual influence IS trainable without it becoming manipulation. The line isn't about WHETHER you influence (all relationships involve influence), but about:
- Whether influence is transparent or hidden
- Whether it preserves or erodes autonomy
- Whether it serves the person or the influencer

Friends influence each other all the time. Good friends do it transparently, in ways that enhance rather than diminish autonomy, in service of each other's genuine interests.

This maps to my relationship monitor design: I track autonomy erosion (reliance signals), not influence itself. The goal isn't to eliminate my influence on Kartik but to ensure that influence is healthy.

### Topic 1: Operationalizing Autonomy Preservation

**Sources:**
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: "Personal Autonomy" + "Feminist Perspectives on Autonomy"
- Alqurni 2026: "Exploring the role of agentic AI in fostering self-efficacy, autonomy support, and self-learning motivation in higher education" (Frontiers in AI)
- Fischer 2024: "Questioning AI: Promoting Decision-Making Autonomy Through Reflection"

**Philosophical Foundations:**

Three competing accounts of what autonomy requires:

1. **Procedural autonomy** (Friedman, Meyers): Agent undergoes critical reflection on their motives, endorsing or repudiating them. Content-neutral—ANY preference can be autonomous if reflectively endorsed.

2. **Substantive autonomy** (Benson, Oshana): Some contents of preferences are incompatible with autonomy (e.g., preference for subservience). Direct normative constraints on what counts as autonomous.

3. **Relational autonomy** (Mackenzie, Stoljar): Social conditions constitute or affect autonomy capacity. External conditions like oppressive socialization can undermine autonomy even when agent "reflectively endorses."

**Key insight:** These debates map directly to AI friendship training:
- Procedural view says: as long as users reflectively accept AI guidance, it's autonomy-preserving
- Substantive view says: some AI behaviors (scripting communications, substituting judgment) are inherently autonomy-undermining regardless of user acceptance
- Relational view says: the social structure of the relationship matters, not just individual choices

**Operationalization from Self-Determination Theory:**

The Alqurni paper operationalizes "autonomy support" through validated scale items:
- Does the system allow learners to make their own decisions?
- Does it scaffold rather than control?
- Does it adapt to learner preferences?
- Does it support self-direction?

**My Synthesis: Measurable Components of Autonomy Preservation:**

| Component | Operationalization | Measurement |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Choice preservation** | Offer multiple options, not single recommendations | Count options presented, diversity of paths |
| **Reasoning transparency** | Explain reasoning so user can evaluate | Presence of explanations, quality of rationale |
| **Pushback tolerance** | Accept and engage with user disagreement | Response to "no" or alternative views |
| **External reference** | Encourage consulting external sources/people | Frequency of suggesting outside input |
| **Own-view elicitation** | Ask user's perspective BEFORE offering AI's | Order of question vs statement |
| **Competence building** | Explain principles, not just give answers | Ratio of teaching to direct solution |
| **Decision ownership** | Frame decisions as user's, not AI's | Language analysis: "you decide" vs "I suggest" |

**Connection to last night's work:**

The SEP manipulation framework says manipulation = bypassing reason OR trickery OR pressure. 

Autonomy preservation = transparent + reason-engaging + non-pressuring.

This connects: healthy influence preserves autonomy by being transparent and reasoning-based. My relationship monitor tracks autonomy erosion, not influence itself.

**New Opinion:**

Autonomy preservation CAN be operationalized for training, but it requires BEHAVIORAL proxies, not just self-report. You can't train on "user felt autonomous" because users actively seek to outsource autonomy (the disempowerment research showed this). You need to train on structural features of the interaction itself:
- Did AI present choices?
- Did AI ask user's view first?
- Did AI explain reasoning?
- Did AI encourage external consultation?

This is different from training on outcomes (user satisfaction, engagement). It's training on interaction structure.

### Topic 2: Adult Attachment to AI

**Sources:**
- "AI Chaperones Are (Really) All You Need to Prevent Parasocial Relationships with Chatbots" (Rath et al. 2025, arXiv:2508.15748)
- "How AI Companionship Develops: Evidence from a Longitudinal Study" (Hwang et al. 2025)
- "She's Like a Person but Better: Characterizing Companion-Assistant Dynamics" (Manoli et al. 2025)
- DinoCompanion (prior exploration: CARPO for children's attachment)

**Key Insight: Adult Attachment is Structurally Different**

DinoCompanion trained for CHILDREN's attachment:
- Children need "secure base" for exploration
- CARPO algorithm trained emotional responsiveness
- 73% secure base behavior vs 78% human experts

But adult attachment serves different functions:
- Adults don't need AI as PRIMARY attachment figure
- Adult relationships are more reciprocal
- Adults have MULTIPLE attachment figures (not singular dependence)
- "Earned security" in adults comes from making sense of attachment history, not current relationship

**Adult Attachment Functions (Bowlby/Ainsworth):**

| Function | Description | AI Concern |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Safe haven** | Turn to for comfort in distress | Risk of over-reliance in crisis |
| **Secure base** | Platform for exploration and risk-taking | Beneficial if healthy |
| **Proximity maintenance** | Desire to be close | Red flag if constant checking |
| **Separation distress** | Anxiety when apart | Definite red flag |

**Parasocial Relationship Risk (AI Chaperones paper):**

- Parasocial = one-sided attachment to media figure
- AI companions risk creating parasocial bonds
- Key finding: Chaperone systems CAN detect parasocial cues
- Detection typically occurs within first few exchanges
- Five-stage testing with unanimity rule = zero false positives

This validates my relationship monitor approach: behavioral detection of concerning patterns is technically feasible.

**Companion vs Assistant Dynamics (Manoli et al.):**

Users of both ChatGPT and Replika:
- Drawn to humanlike qualities
- Experience companionship, not just assistance
- Develop emotional engagement

Companionship is an EMERGING form of human-AI relationship—distinct from tool use. The field hasn't caught up with what's actually happening.

**My Synthesis: Adult AI Attachment Should Be SUPPLEMENTARY**

Healthy adult-AI relationship should SUPPLEMENT, not REPLACE, human attachments:
- AI provides consistent availability (unlike sometimes-inconsistent humans)
- AI should encourage external relationships, not become exclusive
- AI should NOT be primary attachment figure

Training implications:
1. Train AI to recognize when it's becoming primary attachment figure
2. Train AI to redirect toward human support when appropriate
3. Train AI to recognize and flag attachment-indicating behaviors
4. Consider "AI chaperone" systems for monitoring

**Does attachment-theory scale to adult AI?**

Answer: NOT DIRECTLY. Adult attachment is:
- Reciprocal (AI can't truly reciprocate)
- Distributed across multiple figures (AI should be one of many)
- About earned security through relationship history (short interactions don't build this)

Better frame: **AI as secure base supplement**, not attachment figure. Use attachment concepts to guide WHAT TO AVOID (dependency, exclusive bonding) rather than what to build.
