## Interview Process (4 Stages)

### Stage 1: Application + Recruiter Screen
- Resume review
- Basic fit assessment
- Why Jane Street? Why this role?

### Stage 2: Online Assessment
- Probability or coding assessment (role-dependent)
- Tests quantitative reasoning, not finance knowledge

### Stage 3: Technical Phone/Video Screens (2-3 rounds)
- Problem-solving tasks
- Simpler than onsite but still rigorous
- Emphasizes *intuition* around probability and quantitative reasoning

### Stage 4: Final Onsite / Virtual Super-Day
- Multiple interviews in one day
- Mix of technical, case study, and behavioral
- May include mock trading or strategy simulations

### Core Skills
1. **Quantitative reasoning** - probability, expected value, Fermi estimation
2. **Communication** - can you explain your thinking clearly?

## Probability Concepts to Review

These come up frequently in Jane Street interviews:

1. **Expected Value**
   - E[X] = Σ x·P(x)
   - When is it worth taking a bet?

2. **Bayes' Theorem**
   - P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A) / P(B)
   - Updating beliefs with new information

3. **Basic Probability**
   - Independence vs. conditional probability
   - Combinations and permutations

4. **Fermi Estimation**
   - How many piano tuners in Chicago?
   - Break down, estimate, sanity check

5. **Game Theory Basics**
   - Nash equilibrium intuition
   - Zero-sum vs. positive-sum

6. **Market Making Intuition**
   - Bid-ask spreads
   - When/why would a market maker widen spreads?

## Sample Questions to Practice

### Probability
- You flip a fair coin until you get two heads in a row. What's the expected number of flips?
- You have a biased coin. You want to generate fair 50/50 outcomes. How?
- In a room of 23 people, what's the probability two share a birthday?

### Market Intuition (Don't Need Finance Background)
- Why would someone pay to have certainty vs. taking a gamble with higher expected value?
- If a stock has gone up 5 days in a row, what do you think happens tomorrow?
- You're selling lemonade. How do you set the price?

### Strategy
- You have 10 minutes to decide whether to make a trade. What information do you need?
- How would you test if a trading strategy is working?

### Communication
- Explain a complex technical concept to someone non-technical
- Walk through how you'd approach [ambiguous problem]

## How to Position Your Experience

### Microsoft/Copilot
**Frame as:** "I've shipped AI products to 200 million users. I understand what it means to build systems that work at scale, handle edge cases gracefully, and maintain reliability under pressure. Trading systems have similar constraints — they need to be robust when things don't go as expected."

### Tailr/Technical Work
**Frame as:** "I built a system that uses embeddings and retrieval to optimize resumes. This involved working with probabilistic matching, understanding when confidence is high vs. low, and building interfaces that help users make decisions under uncertainty."

## Prep Checklist

### Week 1: Foundations
- [ ] Review probability basics (expected value, Bayes, combinations)
- [ ] Practice 5 Fermi estimation problems
- [ ] Read about market-making fundamentals (Wikipedia is fine)
- [ ] Work through 3-5 probability puzzles

### Week 2: Application
- [ ] Practice market-making scenarios (spreads, inventory)
- [ ] Time yourself on quantitative problems (no more than 10 min each)
- [ ] Do mock interviews with a friend (they read questions, you solve aloud)
- [ ] Practice narrating your thought process

### Week 3: Polish
- [ ] Refine your "why Jane Street" story
- [ ] Prepare questions to ask
- [ ] Review your notes and approaches
- [ ] Rest well before interviews
